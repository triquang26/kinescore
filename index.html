KineScore

From pixels to a robot's true motion — grounded in its own mechanics, so the pose never drifts and generated-video physics can't be faked.

KineScore reads a robot's true 3-D motion straight out of ordinary video: the exact bend of every joint, snapped onto the robot's real mechanical skeleton. The popular way to do this is to guess the movement between frames and add the movements up — but small mistakes pile up and the pose slowly drifts. We instead read the pose directly, guided by the robot's own engineering model, so there is nothing to add up and nothing to drift. It lands closer to the truth than the popular action-guessing methods (IDM and LAPA), and needs far less training data. And because the reading is honest and grounded in real hardware, it doubles as a physics check for AI-generated robot video that can never be fooled into calling genuine footage the fake.

The problem

To use video of a robot, you first have to recover what it physically did

More and more, robots are trained and tested from video — learning a skill by watching, or being graded inside an AI-generated “world model” that dreams up what the robot would see. All of it rests on one hidden step: turning the pixels back into what the robot's body actually did — the exact pose of every joint, in every frame. Get that wrong and everything built on top is wrong too.

The common recipe is to learn the movement between two frames — an “action” — and then add those movements up to rebuild the pose. It is like pacing out a dark room blindfolded: each step is a little off, and the errors pile into a growing drift. Newer methods (LAPA) learn those movements with no labels at all, which is cheaper but blurs the pose even more. We take the opposite route — read the pose directly and snap it onto the robot's real skeleton, so there is nothing to add up and nothing to drift. That faithful pose is what lets us, further down, judge a generated clip's physics with checks that come from the robot's engineering model — so real footage can never be ranked below a fake.

What we ask

Four questions, in plain words

RQ1

Can we read a robot's real pose from ordinary video — accurately enough to trust it?

Yes. The 3-D skeleton read from raw pixels lands about 19 mm from the true one — roughly a fingertip — and the reader also says how sure it is on every frame.

RQ2

Is reading the pose directly better than the popular “guess-the-action” methods (IDM, LAPA)?

Yes — by a wide margin. Because our reader is tied to the robot's own mechanics, it lands several times closer to the true skeleton than IDM or LAPA, and it gets there with far less training data.

RQ3

Does a pose this faithful let us judge generated video's physics without ever being fooled?

Yes. The physical checks come from the robot's own engineering model, not from a library of “normal-looking” videos. By construction, real footage can never be scored below a generated clip.

RQ4

How, exactly, do today's generated robot videos break physics?

Not with impossible poses — today's generators keep joints legal. They break physics in time: the motion judders (about 27% jerkier than real) and limbs subtly change length (about 37% more distortion).

How it works

From pixels to a physical verdict, in four steps

The reader is not a generic pose network bolted on after the fact — it is built around the robot's own kinematic model. Every output maps to a specific degree of freedom in the URDF (the left arm, the right arm, the waist), and each one is given its own attention over the image, so the reader looks at the body part that joint actually controls. The physics checks downstream therefore rest on a pose read the way the robot is really built.

1

Look at the frame

Each frame passes through a frozen, pre-trained vision model. Because it is frozen — never re-trained on our videos — it cannot secretly memorise what real robot footage looks like and quietly help the score cheat.

2

Read the joint angles — one attention per joint

A small predictor turns those features into the robot's actual joint angles, laid out exactly as in the URDF: the left arm, the right arm, and the waist, one output per degree of freedom. It is structured by the robot's own kinematics — each joint carries its own attention query over the image (the saliency above), so a joint reads its own limb rather than the whole frame being squeezed through one black-box guess. Crucially it reports the raw angle even when it is physically impossible, so a bad prediction stays visible instead of being quietly clipped back into the legal range.

3

Say how sure it is

For every joint it also outputs a confidence. Frames where the view is blurry or blocked come with lower confidence, so they can be set aside rather than trusted blindly.

4

Rebuild the real robot

Those angles drive the robot's exact 3-D model (the manufacturer's CAD/URDF design). That gives the true position of every part in space — and once we have 3-D positions, the physical rules become simple geometry.

The method, and why it wins

Everything after that is what a faithful pose unlocks — an honest physics check for AI-generated video.

The reconstructed skeleton tracks the real robot

Setup. We compare the 3-D skeleton read from raw video against the robot's true skeleton on held-out real recordings.

Result. The skeleton reconstructed from pixels lands about 19 mm from the true 3-D skeleton on held-out real clips.

What it means. About 19 mm is roughly the width of a fingertip — small enough that everything built on top of it (the physics checks) rests on a faithful pose. The reader also reports its confidence, so the few frames it is unsure about need not be trusted.

Experiment 2 · The core result — our reader vs. IDM & LAPA (RQ2)

Reading pose through the robot's model beats learning to integrate actions

Setup. All methods are trained the same way — head-only on the same frozen DINOv3 image features, the same GR1 robot data, the same held-out validation split, and scored with the same 3-D keypoint-error metric (millimetres from the true skeleton). The only thing that differs is the learning signal / architecture. We also vary how much labelled data each one is given — 10%, 40%, 100% of the training episodes — to see who needs more data.

The alternatives, and how each recovers a pose

  • Ours — FK readout: built around the robot's kinematic model. A frozen vision model gives image features; a set of attention maps — one per URDF degree of freedom, in the robot's own left-arm / right-arm / waist layout — each read the limb that joint controls, and output the joint's absolute angle plus a confidence. The pose is then read straight through the robot's exact forward-kinematics chain (its CAD/URDF model). It never integrates anything, so there is nothing to drift.
  • Direct readout (no kinematic structure): the same frozen features fed to a plain predictor that regresses the absolute joint angles per frame — but without the per-joint attention, the forward-kinematics keypoint training, or the confidence. It isolates how much the kinematic structure itself is worth.
  • IDM-absolute: reads a pair of consecutive frames and predicts the absolute next pose directly. A control: it uses a frame pair like an inverse-dynamics model but still reads an absolute pose, so it never has to integrate — this separates “the drift” from “the frame-pair input” as the source of error.
  • Learned IDM (inverse dynamics): reads a pair of frames and predicts the action between them — the change in joint angles. To get an actual pose you must start from a known pose and add up these predicted changes frame after frame; tiny per-step errors accumulate, so the pose drifts away from the truth over a clip.
  • LAPA (latent action pretraining): a popular label-efficient recipe. First, with no labels, it learns a small dictionary of discrete “latent actions” that explain how one frame turns into the next (a vector-quantised code plus a forward model). Then a tiny probe, trained on a little labelled data, maps each latent action to a joint change — which again must be integrated, and the unsupervised latent carries only limited information about the true pose, so it both drifts and plateaus.

Result. Reading the pose directly through the robot's model lands about 21 mm from the true skeleton on full data — roughly 4× closer than a learned IDM (92 mm) and 5× closer than LAPA (112 mm). It is also far more data-efficient: on just 10% of the labels it already reads about 53 mm, beating IDM and LAPA at any budget. The two controls show why. Dropping the integration alone (IDM-absolute, 36 mm) recovers most of the gap — so the “add it up” drift is the biggest single cost — and the rest comes from the kinematic grounding: a plain direct readout with no robot structure reaches 38 mm, still about 1.8× worse than ours. And tellingly, LAPA gets worse with more data (83 → 112 mm): its unsupervised latent simply doesn't carry the true pose, so more labels can't rescue it.

Ours here is trained at a matched budget against every baseline; our fully-trained production reader reaches 19 mm (the dotted line in the chart below). LAPA's 10/40/100% counts only its labelled probe data — it also uses all clips unlabelled in its first stage, which is why more labels barely move it.

What it means. Our reader is not a generic pose network — it is wired to the robot's own kinematics, every output a specific joint with its own attention, and the pose is read straight through the exact forward-kinematics chain, so it never integrates and has nothing to drift; and because each joint is pinned to its own limb it reaches a faithful pose from little data. The inverse-dynamics family instead learns a free-form action and has to add it up from a known start, so small errors compound into large drift over a clip; and a purely learned latent action (LAPA) barely encodes the true pose, so it plateaus no matter how much you integrate. That is why, at every data budget, the constrained readout lands several times closer to the true skeleton — the grounding in the robot's engineering model is exactly what buys the accuracy.

What a faithful pose unlocks

What the ruler actually checks

Once the pose is read faithfully, it doubles as a physics ruler for video. A measurement is only trustworthy if you can see what it looks at. So for every check we put a clean, physically fine pose beside a deliberate violation, and show exactly what the number reads in each case. The first five are pure physics rules — geometry & dynamics on the robot's own model, with nothing learned about which poses are “typical.” The last is the model's own honesty about how sure it is.

Mechanical feasibility — rules from the robot's own model

Do solid parts of the body overlap?

physics rule · nothing learned

In real life: an arm passing straight through the chest, which a solid body can never do.

what it checks Checks whether any two solid parts of the body pass through each other in 3-D — for example an arm through the chest.

a violation looks like Folding the arms into the torso makes the parts overlap by about 12 cm; a genuine recorded pose overlaps by exactly zero.

Does the robot's weight stay over its feet?

physics rule · nothing learned

In real life: leaning so far you topple over instead of staying upright.

what it checks Measures how much margin there is before the robot's weight would fall outside the area under its feet.

a violation looks like A hard lean shrinks that margin; on this light-armed robot it never actually tips, and we report that honestly.

Is every joint within its real limits?

physics rule · nothing learned

In real life: a joint bent further than the real motor allows, like an elbow hinging backwards.

what it checks Checks each joint against the angles the real hardware can physically reach, using the manufacturer's model.

a violation looks like An impossible pose pushes four joints past their limits (drawn in red); the real pose stays fully in range.

Motion over time, and the model's confidence

Does the motion flow smoothly over time?

physics rule · nothing learned

In real life: motion that stutters and snaps instead of flowing the way a real motor moves.

what it checks Measures how abruptly the arms change speed from frame to frame — smooth motion scores low, juddering motion scores high.

a violation looks like A generated clip judders far more than the real clip it is paired with, here roughly two-and-a-half times as much.

Could the real motors even produce this motion?

physics rule · nothing learned

In real life: a joint whipped so fast that the motor simply cannot push that hard.

what it checks Runs the robot's full inverse dynamics — every link's mass and inertia from the URDF — to work out the joint torque the motion demands, and compares it against the motor's rated limit (in N·m).

a violation looks like Real motion uses only about 5% of the motor's rated torque; the same motion jerked hard spikes past 200% — beyond what any real motor could deliver.

How sure is the reading on this frame?

the model's own confidence

In real life: the model saying 'I can't quite tell here' instead of pretending to be certain.

what it checks For each joint, the pose reader also reports how confident it is, so unreliable frames can be set aside rather than trusted blindly.

a violation looks like On a blurry or blocked frame its stated uncertainty grows on the hard joints — a flag, not a silent guess.

The evaluator in action

What a faithful pose reveals about generated video

Now the payoff. With the pose read faithfully — and the physics rulers defined above — we point them at real AI-generated robot video and let them report, honestly, what actually breaks.

Experiment 3 · A fair ruler (RQ3)

Real footage is never scored below a fake

Setup. On real recordings we ask whether the physics checks ever rate real footage as less valid than generated footage.

Result. On real footage, the two feasibility checks that don't depend on frame rate read the same for real and generated — a coin-flip, never worse for the real clip. (A third check, balance, correctly rates real footage as more stable.)

What it means. This is the whole point of building the ruler from physics rules rather than a learned sense of “normal.” A rule can never decide that genuine footage is less physically valid than a fake — the failure that traps learned scores simply cannot happen here.

Experiment 4 · A dial, not an alarm

The reading rises smoothly as a pose gets worse

Setup. We corrupt real poses by growing amounts, and separately degrade the video quality, and watch how the readings respond.

Result. As a pose is corrupted more and more, the violation reading rises smoothly with it (in almost perfect order for overlap, in perfect order for joint limits). And as the video quality is degraded, the model's stated uncertainty climbs in step (in perfect order).

What it means. The ruler is a dial, not an on/off light. A slightly-off pose reads slightly bad; a very-off pose reads very bad. That means the number is meaningful, not just a yes/no alarm — and the model gets honestly less confident exactly when the picture gets harder to read.

Experiment 5 · The main finding (RQ4)

Generated motion is measurably jerkier than real

Setup. For each generated clip we compare it against its own ground-truth clip — same scene, same task — so nothing but the generation differs.

Result. Compared frame-for-frame against its own real counterpart, every generated clip is jerkier. Typical jerk rises from about 15.7 for the real clip to about 21.3 for the generated one — a gap of roughly 5.5 per episode, across 116 episodes. In 99 episodes out of 100 the generated clip was the jerkier one.

And the extra jerk added by the speed-up tricks themselves — almost nothing on top of what generation already costs:

What it means. By comparing each generated clip only to its own ground truth, nothing else differs — same scene, same task — so the extra jerk can only come from the generation. In plain terms, a difference this large essentially never happens by chance (the odds against it are astronomical, p ≈ 2×10-20). That gap is also larger than the tool's own frame-to-frame wobble (about 3.7), so it is a real signal, not measurement noise. On frame-rate-matched footage — the fair basis, since raw jerk depends on frame rate — generated motion runs about 27% jerkier than real (the raw 15.7 → 21.3 pair above is on the un-matched clips), and its limbs distort about 37% more.

Experiment 6 · More ways the ruler can look

Rigidity and principled smoothness confirm the story

Setup. Beyond raw jerk, we read several more physical rulers on the same clips — limb rigidity, two scale-free smoothness measures (SPARC and dimensionless jerk), and hard checks for impossible speeds and teleports. Each is shown firing or staying quiet in “the ruler in the wild” above; here we put a single number on each — its separation, how reliably it tells a real clip from a generated one (0.5 = a coin-flip, 1.0 = perfect). Two kinds of number appear across these checks and they answer different questions: a magnitude in physical units (millimetres, m/s³) says how large a violation is; a separation score (0.5–1.0) says how reliably a ruler tells real from fake. We keep each in its natural form rather than squashing everything into one 0–1 number — the physical units, read straight off the robot's own model, are the whole point.

Result. Limbs shouldn’t stretch like rubber. On fairly-matched footage, a limb wanders about 4.3 mm in length on real clips but about 5.9 mm on generated ones (averaged over all clips; the worst clips rubber-band by 20–34 mm, as the gallery above shows) — enough for the rigidity check to separate real from generated reliably (67.4% of the time, on a scale where 50% is a coin-flip and 100% is perfect). Two principled, scale-free smoothness measures (SPARC and dimensionless jerk) agree that generated motion is clearly less smooth than real (about 87.9% separation for SPARC). Importantly, these fix a subtlety: the raw jerk number depends on frame rate, so on clips recorded at different speeds it can look misleading — the scale-free measures don’t have that problem.

What it means. The tell is not speed — it is texture. Generated motion reaches the same speeds as real and never breaks a joint limit or teleports, so the velocity and teleport rulers read a coin-flip (~0.50); what separates real from fake is how the motion changes over time — it stutters (smoothness) and its limbs subtly drift in length (rigidity), and the scale-free smoothness measures stay fair even across different frame rates. A single ruler can be gamed; a suite that each measures a different physical property, and honestly reports which ones fire, is much harder to fool. So the 0.50 rows are not filler — they are the proof that the metric is a specific diagnostic that would light up the day a generator does start teleporting, not a blanket “generated = bad” alarm.

Experiment 7 · Smoothness, grounded in the hardware

The judder is real — but is it physically impossible? We can check both

Setup. Jerk in m/s³ has no natural ceiling, so “how jerky is too jerky?” has no absolute answer. So we read the same generated clips two ways. The kinematic way is the usual jerk. The dynamic way runs the robot's full inverse dynamics from its engineering model — every link's mass and inertia from the URDF — to work out the actual joint torque the motion would demand, and compares it against the torque the real motors are rated to deliver: a hard physical limit, in newton-metres.

Result. Both agree the motion is wrong, and together they pin down how wrong. Generated motion is far jerkier than real — the kinematic view separates real from generated perfectly (peak jerk about 41 vs 290–350 m/s³). But converted into torque, that same judder still asks for only about 8–9% of the motors' rated limit (real motion sits near 7%), and never exceeds it. So on this light humanoid the generators' physics failure is a subtle temporal one — unmistakably jerky, yet still physically executable — not a gross impossibility.

What it means. This is what grounding in the robot's own model buys: we can express the same defect in an interpretable kinematic unit and on a scale with a real physical ceiling. The honest reading is that generated motion is clearly jerkier but still within the motors' reach — the tell is temporal texture, not brute infeasibility. The very same dynamic ruler would fire outright the day a generator (or a heavier robot with less torque headroom) pushes a joint past its motor limit.

Experiment 8 · Speed-ups are free

Making the generator faster adds no extra jerk

Setup. Several published methods speed these generators up by caching. We measure the extra jerk each one adds on top of the un-accelerated generator, and rank the methods.

Result.

  • dicache (about 2.3× faster): extra jerk from caching ≈ −0.3 — slightly smoother, essentially none.
  • fastercache (about 1.7× faster): extra jerk from caching ≈ −0.6 — slightly smoother, essentially none.
  • worldcache (about 2.1× faster): extra jerk from caching ≈ −0.4 — slightly smoother, essentially none.

What it means. Set against the roughly +5.5 that generation itself adds, the extra jerk from any speed-up is a rounding error — and often slightly negative, i.e. a touch smoother. Across the methods there is no trend of “faster means jerkier.” The physics cost lives in generating the video, not in accelerating it — so these speed-ups are, for physics, free.

What we conclude

Takeaways

  • Reading the pose directly through the robot's exact kinematic model, instead of learning an action and adding it up, is what makes it both far more accurate and far more data-efficient than the popular inverse-dynamics (IDM) and latent-action (LAPA) baselines.
  • That direct read is accurate enough to trust — about 19 mm from the true 3-D skeleton — and the reader also knows when to doubt itself.
  • Grounding the checks in the robot's engineering model, rather than a learned sense of “normal,” means real footage can never be unfairly ranked below a fake.
  • Today's generated robot videos don't strike impossible poses — they break physics in time: the motion judders (about 27% jerkier) and limbs subtly change length.
  • The speed-up tricks that accelerate these generators are, for physics, free: they add no measurable extra jerk.

Honest limitations

  • On today's generators, the “impossible pose” checks almost never fire — the real signal is temporal (jerk and limb distortion), and we say so plainly.
  • This pass covers one humanoid robot and one family of generators; some cross-settings are still being finished.
  • Comparing across different generators fairly requires matching their frame rates first; that matching is ongoing.

Still running

  • Extra generators are being folded into the benchmark.
  • Standard, widely-used video-quality baselines are under selection for a head-to-head.
